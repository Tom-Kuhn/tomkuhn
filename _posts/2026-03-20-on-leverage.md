The thing that distinguishes a staff engineer from a senior engineer isn't technical depth. Both can have plenty of that. The distinguishing factor is leverage — the ability to multiply impact beyond what you can produce yourself.

Most engineers understand this intellectually. Fewer understand what it actually requires you to give up.

## Output is a trap

Early in a career, output is the metric. Lines of code, tickets closed, features shipped. You get good at producing things, and producing things feels good. It's concrete. It's measurable. It's satisfying in a way that's hard to replicate.

At some point — different for everyone, but usually somewhere in the senior-to-staff transition — the incentive structure inverts. The most valuable thing you can do is often not the thing that produces the most direct output. It's the thing that makes everyone around you more effective.

That's a hard adjustment. It took me longer than I expected.

## What leverage actually looks like

Leverage isn't mysterious. It's mostly a set of mundane practices applied consistently:

**Documentation that gets used.** Not documentation for its own sake. Documentation that answers the question the next engineer would have asked you. Every time you answer a question verbally that you've answered before, you've identified something worth writing down.

**Processes that remove friction.** The RFC template, the deployment checklist, the incident retrospective format. Good process is leverage because it encodes your judgement into something that operates without you.

**Code that other code is built on.** Libraries, platforms, shared abstractions. The return on investment for these is asymmetric — you build once, many teams benefit. The catch is that you don't always get credit for what didn't go wrong.

**People who become better than you expected.** The engineer you mentored who now mentors others. The conversation that changed someone's mental model of a problem. These compound in ways that are almost impossible to attribute, which is perhaps why they're underrated.

## The credit problem

Leverage work is often invisible. When you write a feature, there's a diff. When you help five engineers write better features, there's no diff. When your architectural decision prevents a class of problems, nothing bad happens — and nothing bad happening doesn't show up in a performance review.

This is a structural problem with how most engineering organisations measure impact. I don't have a clean solution to it, other than: find a manager who understands the difference between output and leverage, and help them see what you're contributing.

## The residue question

The question I've started asking myself is: what will remain after I leave?

Code will get rewritten. Systems will get replaced. The things that tend to persist are practices, norms, mental models, and people. The engineers you developed, the culture you reinforced, the way a team thinks about problems.

That's a longer game than most engineers play. But it's the game worth playing.
